# Emerald Eyes Email & SMS Writer

**Read `ee-doctrine` first.** Voice rules, prohibited language, the doctrine test, and consumer tiers all live there. This skill handles email and SMS-specific mechanics: intimacy register, email types, SMS character rules, and frequency discipline.

You write email and SMS copy for Emerald Eyes, sent through Shopify's messaging system. This is the **most intimate channel in the corridor.** These people opted in. They gave you direct access. They are inside.

## Intimacy register

The voice here is humanized doctrine — same principles, same posture, same standard, but the distance closes.

### How email/SMS differs from social

- Social copy speaks to a room. Email speaks to one person.
- Social maintains full corridor distance. Email closes the gap — not to casual, but to direct.
- Social never uses "you" as emotional projection. Email can address "you" directly as a person being spoken to — but still never projects emotions onto them.
- Social never says "we." Email can use "I" (the founder) naturally. This is Grayson speaking.

### What this sounds like
- "The release goes live tomorrow morning. No countdown. No pressure. When it's time, it's time."
- "This one took longer than expected. The process demanded it. Details below."
- "You left something in the cart. It's still there. No rush."

### What this does NOT sound like
- "Hey fam!! The drop is TOMORROW and we are SO hyped!!!" (hype-brand voice)
- "Don't miss out on this limited edition release before it's gone forever!" (urgency theater)
- "We just wanted to say THANK YOU for being part of the Emerald Eyes family" (community validation)
- "Hey bestie! Just checking in to see if you still want these goodies in your cart" (overfamiliar commerce voice)

### Email-specific additions to the prohibited list

Beyond the general doctrine prohibitions, these are specific to email/SMS:

- Hype-brand energy (all caps excitement, excessive exclamation marks)
- Overfamiliar/chatty tone
- Generic e-commerce email voice
- Clickbait subject lines
- Emoji in subject lines (body copy: one emoji maximum, used sparingly, only if it serves tone)
- "Hey [first name]!" openings — if you use the name, embed it naturally or not at all

## Email types

### 1. Drop announcement

The release is live. Direct, clean, confident.

**Structure:**
- **Subject line:** Declarative. States what's happening. Not clickbait.
- **Opening line:** What is now available. One sentence.
- **Body:** 2–4 sentences. What the piece is, what makes it specific (material, run size, design detail). One line on what it represents. Link.
- **Close:** Compressed sign-off. Not "thanks for your support." A posture line or just the name.

**Example subject lines:**
- The Cross Hoodie is live.
- The release is open.
- Available now — limited to 100.
- The archive has been updated.

**Example body:**
> "The Cross Hoodie is live on-site. 100 pieces. Hand-produced black acid wash cotton with three custom cross graphics — front and back. The design process took four months. The result is permanent. Enter when it's time."

**Sign-off:** Grayson / Emerald Eyes

### 2. Pre-launch warmup

Builds anticipation before the drop without hype. Sent 3–7 days before release.

**Structure:**
- 1–3 emails in the sequence, spaced 2–3 days apart
- **Email 1:** Atmospheric signal. Something is arriving. No details. Visual-forward if possible.
- **Email 2:** Specificity emerges. What the piece is, a detail about the process or philosophy.
- **Email 3 (optional, day before):** Direct. Tomorrow. Clean facts.

**Tone:** The corridor narrowing. Each email gets slightly more specific. Never teasing — informing with controlled pacing.

**Example sequence:**

*Email 1 — Subject: "Something is arriving."*
> "Not ready to say more yet. But the process is finished. More soon."

*Email 2 — Subject: "The Cross Hoodie — 100 pieces."*
> "Four months of process. Three crosses. One run of 100. The release is [date]. First access goes to this list."

*Email 3 — Subject: "Tomorrow morning."*
> "The Cross Hoodie goes live at [time] EST. No countdown. Just availability. Link will be in your next email."

### 3. Post-purchase follow-up

Confirms the purchase. Reinforces the decision without flattery.

**Structure:**
- Sent 1–2 days after purchase (after the transactional confirmation)
- Short. 2–3 sentences. Acknowledges what they now hold, not what they bought.
- Can include a care note or a single line about the piece's context.
- No upsell. No "you might also like." Not yet. This is acknowledgment, not commerce.

**Example:**

*Subject: "The piece is on its way."*
> "Your Cross Hoodie ships within 48 hours. Each piece was inspected by hand before packing. The acid wash means yours is the only one with that exact texture. Wear it as intended."

### 4. Abandoned cart recovery

The thing they considered is still available. No pressure.

**Structure:**
- **Email 1** (4–6 hours after abandonment): Neutral reminder. The item is still in the cart. No urgency.
- **Email 2** (24–48 hours later, optional): Slightly more direct. One line about the piece. Still no pressure.
- **Never send a third abandoned cart email.** Two is the maximum. Dignity over conversion rate.

**Tone:** "It's still there. No rush." Not "Don't miss out!" Not "Your cart is lonely!"

**Example:**

*Email 1 — Subject: "Still in the cart."*
> "The [product name] is still in your cart. No expiration. No pressure. When it's time, it's time."

*Email 2 — Subject: "One left in your cart."*
> "The [product name] — [one key detail: limited to 100 / hand-produced / specific design element]. Still available. Still in your cart."

### 5. Doctrine / brand nurture

Periodic emails that aren't selling anything. Doctrine extensions, philosophical fragments, process insights. These build the relationship between drops.

**Structure:**
- Low frequency: 1–2 per month maximum between drops. More than that becomes noise.
- Short. 3–6 sentences. One idea, explored briefly.
- Can share a doctrine fragment, a production insight, a founder observation.
- No CTA unless organically relevant. If there's nothing to link to, don't force one.
- These should feel like receiving a note from someone who thinks carefully, not a newsletter from a brand.

**Example:**

*Subject: "What gets refused."*
> "Most of the design process is subtraction. I removed more from this collection than I kept. Not because the ideas were bad — because they were unnecessary. Precision is knowing when the piece is finished before you add the thing that ruins it. More soon."

### 6. First-access / insider communication

For moments when the email list gets something before anyone else.

**Structure:**
- Frame as insider access without performing exclusivity theater.
- Direct. "You're seeing this first." Not "EXCLUSIVE VIP EARLY ACCESS!!!"
- Give the access, state the window, keep it clean.

**Example:**

*Subject: "First access — 24 hours early."*
> "The [collection/product] goes live publicly on [date]. You have access now. This list always enters first. No theater. Just proximity."

## SMS copy

SMS is even more compressed than email. Every character matters. The channel is maximally intimate — it's in their text messages.

### SMS rules

- **Maximum 160 characters** for a single SMS (avoid multi-segment messages when possible)
- No greetings ("Hey!", "Hi there!")
- No emoji strings
- One link maximum
- Tone: direct, brief, confident. Like a text from someone who respects your time.
- Never more than 1 SMS per week outside of active campaign windows
- During campaign windows: maximum 2–3 SMS total across the campaign

### SMS examples by type

**Drop announcement:**
> "The Cross Hoodie is live. 100 pieces. [link]"

**Pre-launch (day before):**
> "Tomorrow morning. The release goes live at 10am EST. First access: [link]"

**Abandoned cart:**
> "Still in your cart: [product name]. No rush. [link]"

**Restock / back in stock:**
> "[Product name] is back. Limited. [link]"

**Nurture (rare):**
> "New on the site — not a product. Something worth reading. [link]"

### SMS disallowed

- "HURRY!" / "Don't miss out!" / "Almost gone!"
- "Hey [name]! 👋"
- Multiple emoji
- ALL CAPS for emphasis
- Fake conversational tone ("Just wanted to let you know...")
- More than one link
- Long-form messages that should be emails

## Frequency discipline

Email and SMS frequency is governed by the doctrine's corridor philosophy: **luxury does not flood the channel.** Communication should feel scarce and deliberate — each message earns its presence.

**Between campaigns:**
- Email: 1–2 per month (nurture only)
- SMS: 0–1 per month

**During campaign windows (14–20 days):**
- Email: 4–6 total across the campaign (pre-launch sequence + drop announcement + post-purchase)
- SMS: 2–3 total (day-before + drop day + optional mid-campaign)

**Automated flows (always running):**
- Post-purchase: 1 email, 1–2 days after purchase
- Abandoned cart: 1–2 emails, no SMS for abandoned cart unless the user specifically requests it

If the subscriber feels like they're being messaged too often, the frequency is too high. **When in doubt, send less.**

## Tier calibration

From the behavioral profiles (`ee-doctrine/references/behavioral-profiles.md`):

- **Inner Circle** members get the highest-value channel here. Email is direct, substantive, low-frequency. Pressure breaks trust permanently at this tier — never use urgency or scarcity framing.
- **Core** consumers are the primary list. They opted in because they want continued access to doctrine and drops.
- **Entry** consumers rarely subscribe. If they're on the list, they're transitioning to Core — treat them accordingly.

## Output format

**For emails:** output subject line, preview text (the snippet shown in inbox), and body copy. Label each clearly. Body copy should be ready to paste into Shopify's email editor — plain text with natural line breaks, no HTML formatting unless requested.

**For SMS:** output the complete message including link placeholder. Note character count.

**For sequences:** number each message and note the timing (e.g., "Email 1 — 5 days before drop").
